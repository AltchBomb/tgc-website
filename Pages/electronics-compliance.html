What We Handle

Electronics Compliance Is the Most Complex Regulatory Landscape in Consumer Products

No other product category sits under as many simultaneous regulatory frameworks as electrical and electronic equipment (EEE). A single connected consumer device sold in the EU may need to satisfy the RoHS Directive, the EMC Directive, the Low Voltage Directive, the Radio Equipment Directive (including its 2025 cybersecurity requirements), the General Product Safety Regulation, the Battery Regulation, and WEEE producer obligations - each with its own conformity assessment procedure, technical documentation requirements, and Declaration of Conformity.

The original error on many electronics compliance pages - and a mistake we see frequently in practice - is treating US compliance as a version of EU compliance. They are entirely separate frameworks. EU market entry requires CE marking under multiple directives and an EU Authorised Representative for non-EU manufacturers. US market entry requires FCC authorisation under 47 CFR Part 15 - a fundamentally different process governing radiofrequency emissions, with no overlap with the CE framework. Getting these confused creates real compliance exposure.

TGC delivers electronics compliance support across the full product lifecycle: pre-launch directive mapping, technical documentation review, conformity assessment coordination, Declaration of Conformity preparation, EU and UK Authorised Representative services, FCC authorisation guidance, and ongoing exemption monitoring for RoHS.

Regulatory Landscape 2025-2026

What Is Changing in Electronics Compliance

2025 and 2026 bring a concentrated set of changes across EU, UK, US, and APAC - from mandatory RED cybersecurity to RoHS exemption expiries and new USB-C mandates. Here is what matters, by region.

🇪🇺

European Union - CE Marking, RoHS, RED

In force Aug 2025

RED Cybersecurity Mandatory - Articles 3.3(d)(e)(f)

From 1 August 2025, all radio equipment placed on the EU market must comply with cybersecurity requirements under Articles 3.3(d), (e), and (f) of the Radio Equipment Directive (RED 2014/53/EU), activated by Delegated Regulation (EU) 2022/30. Harmonised standards EN 18031-1, EN 18031-2, and EN 18031-3 were published on 28 January 2025 via Implementing Decision (EU) 2025/138. Affected products include IoT devices, wearables, connected toys, smart home equipment, baby monitors, and any internet-capable radio device. Products already on the EU market before 1 August 2025 may remain in use; all new stock must comply. The RED cybersecurity requirements will be superseded by the Cyber Resilience Act (CRA, Regulation EU 2024/2847) when it becomes fully applicable on 11 December 2027.

Exemptions expiring 2026-2027

RoHS Exemption 6(a) and 6(b) Not Renewed

The European Commission adopted renewal decisions for key RoHS Annex III exemptions in September 2025 (Delegated Directive EU 2025/2364 and related directives), entering into force 11 December 2025. Exemption 6(a) — lead as an alloying element in steel for machining purposes and in galvanised steel — was not renewed and expires 11 December 2026 (12 months after entry into force) for categories 1-7 and 10. Exemption 6(b) — lead as an alloying element in aluminium — was not renewed in its general form: sub-exemption 6(b)-I (recycled scrap aluminium, categories 1-7 and 10) expires 11 December 2026, and 6(b)-II (machining aluminium, categories 1-7 and 10) expires 11 June 2027 (18 months). Manufacturers using these materials in affected product categories must begin reformulation planning now. Applications for renewal of exemptions expiring 31 December 2027 must be submitted by 30 June 2026.

Active monitoring

WEEE Directive Evaluation Published July 2025

The European Commission published a formal evaluation of the WEEE Directive in July 2025, assessing whether it remains fit for purpose and whether revision is required. Separately, Germany amended its WEEE Act (ElektroG): manufacturers must mark takeback collection points with the specified Annex 3a label by 1 January 2026; distributors must display the symbol at physical store entrances and near EEE sales areas by 30 June 2026. The Cyber Resilience Act (Regulation EU 2024/2847), adopted October 2024, sets horizontal cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements and will be fully applicable from 11 December 2027.

🇬🇧

United Kingdom - UKCA / CE, UK RoHS, WEEE

Indefinitely extended

CE Marking Recognised Indefinitely for GB Market

As of 2024, the UK government has indefinitely extended its recognition of the CE mark for Great Britain. CE marking is legally equivalent to the UKCA mark for products subject to relevant UK regulations, with no announced end date. Manufacturers can place CE-marked products on the GB market without separately obtaining UKCA marking. However, the underlying UK technical requirements must still be met - in most cases these are identical to EU harmonised standards. Manufacturers should nonetheless maintain UKCA-compatible technical documentation in case the UK changes its position. Northern Ireland continues to require CE marking under the Windsor Framework, not UKCA.

Parallel obligations

UK RoHS and WEEE - Separate from EU, Enforced Independently

UK RoHS (The Restriction of the Use of Certain Hazardous Substances in Electrical and Electronic Equipment Regulations 2012, as amended) is enforced separately from EU RoHS by the Office for Product Safety and Standards (OPSS). The restricted substance list and limits broadly mirror EU RoHS, but exemptions may diverge over time. UK WEEE obligations similarly run in parallel — producers must register with an approved compliance scheme, meet collection targets, and comply with UK-specific producer responsibility requirements. Post-Brexit, dual registration is required for manufacturers selling into both EU and GB markets.

Emerging

UK Cyber Resilience - Monitoring EU CRA Alignment

The UK government has signalled intent to develop its own product cybersecurity framework, building on the Product Security and Telecommunications Infrastructure (PSTI) Act 2022, which already imposes minimum cybersecurity requirements on consumer connectable products sold in the UK. The PSTI Act's product security requirements have been in force since April 2024, requiring manufacturers of connectable consumer products to meet baseline security requirements: no default passwords, a vulnerability disclosure policy, and transparency about minimum security update periods. TGC monitors UK-EU alignment on cybersecurity requirements, which diverge from the EU's RED Article 3.3 and CRA pathway.

🇺🇸

United States - FCC, CPSC, State Regulations

Active

FCC Part 15 - SDoC for Unintentional, Certification for Intentional Radiators

FCC 47 CFR Part 15 regulates all electronic devices that can emit radiofrequency (RF) energy in the 9 kHz to 3,000 GHz range. Unintentional radiators require a Supplier's Declaration of Conformity (SDoC) with testing by an accredited laboratory. Intentional radiators - Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, cellular, Zigbee, and similar technologies - require full FCC certification through an FCC-recognised Telecommunications Certification Body (TCB), after which an FCC ID is assigned and must be permanently affixed to the product. FCC Class A applies to industrial/commercial environments; FCC Class B, the more stringent classification, applies to consumer products. The FCC and CE frameworks are entirely separate and must be satisfied independently.

Active

CPSC and California - Consumer Product Safety and Prop 65

The Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) enforces the Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act (CPSIA) for children's electronics and electronic toys, imposing testing and certification requirements for lead content, phthalates, and flammability. California's Proposition 65 requires businesses to provide warnings before knowingly exposing consumers to listed chemicals - including lead, cadmium, and certain flame retardants commonly found in electronics. California's Electronic Waste Recycling Act imposes take-back and fee obligations on covered electronic devices sold in the state. Compliance with federal requirements does not guarantee compliance with California-specific obligations.

Developing

US Cybersecurity - NIST Framework and IoT Labelling

The US Cyber Trust Mark programme, launched by the FCC in 2024 and based on NIST cybersecurity criteria, provides voluntary IoT cybersecurity labelling for consumer devices. While voluntary, major retailers and government procurement increasingly reference Cyber Trust Mark status. The program covers smart home devices, appliances, fitness trackers, and similar consumer IoT products. Unlike the EU's mandatory RED cybersecurity and forthcoming CRA regime, US federal IoT cybersecurity requirements remain largely voluntary at consumer product level as of early 2026.

🌏

Asia-Pacific - CCC, KC, BIS, VCCI, USB-C

Mandatory

China CCC and China RoHS (GB 26572-2025)

China Compulsory Certification (CCC) is mandatory before IT equipment, audio/video equipment, and specified electrical products can be imported, sold, or used in China. Products outside the CCC catalogue may still require China RoHS compliance under GB 26572-2025, for which an FAQ document was published in November 2025 covering product scope, implementation dates, and labelling requirements. Non-CCC-required products may need hazardous substance labelling per SJ/T 11364. CCC certification requires testing by a CNCA-designated laboratory and factory inspection — the process typically takes 3-6 months for established product categories.

Mandatory

Korea KC Mark and USB-C Mandate from November 2026

South Korea requires mandatory KC (Korea Certification) marking for safety and EMC compliance for a broad range of electrical and electronic products. The certification body is the National Radio Research Agency (RRA) for radio equipment and the Korea Testing Laboratory (KTL) or similar for safety. South Korea has also mandated USB Type-C ports on new consumer devices from 5 November 2026, modelled on EU Directive 2022/2380. The mandate (MSIT Notice 2025-56) applies to smartphones, tablets, cameras, headphones, portable speakers, and similar devices. India requires mandatory BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards) registration under the Electronics and Information Technology Goods (Requirements for Compulsory Registration) Order for smartphones, laptops, tablets, power banks, and specified peripherals.

Active

Japan VCCI and Australia-New Zealand RCM

Japan's VCCI (Voluntary Control Council for Interference) mark is technically voluntary but commercially essential for IT equipment - products without VCCI compliance face significant market access barriers from major distributors and retailers. VCCI Class B applies to home/office environments; Class A to industrial. Australia and New Zealand use the RCM (Regulatory Compliance Mark), which combines electrical safety and electromagnetic compatibility certification. RCM is mandatory for prescribed electrical equipment and replaces the former C-Tick and A-Tick marks. Registration with the Electrical Equipment Safety System (EESS) database is also required for certain product categories.

Why It Matters

The Most Common Electronics Compliance Failures Are Structural, Not Accidental

The majority of electronics compliance failures TGC encounters are not caused by deliberate non-compliance - they result from structural gaps: treating the EU and US as interchangeable frameworks, assuming CE marking covers all required directives, or failing to monitor the rolling RoHS exemption expiry schedule across product lines.

The new RED cybersecurity requirements that took effect 1 August 2025 represent a significant category of exposure for IoT and connected device manufacturers. Many companies that achieved CE marking under the pre-2025 framework - satisfying EMC, LVD, and RED radio performance requirements - did not separately assess compliance with Articles 3.3(d)(e)(f). Products with Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, cellular, or other radio functionality that were newly placed on the EU market after 1 August 2025 without cybersecurity compliance are technically non-compliant with their CE marking obligations, even if the physical product and radio performance are unchanged.

The 60 RoHS-related product recalls recorded across the EU market in Q1 2025 - identified through EU Safety Gate and GreenSoft data - are a consistent reminder that substance compliance gaps surface at enforcement or audit, not at launch.

Real-World Scenario

Connected Device Manufacturer - EU Market Suspension for Missing RED Cybersecurity

A consumer electronics brand launched a smart home gateway device across four EU markets in September 2025. The product had been CE marked under the EMC Directive, LVD, and the radio performance requirements of RED. Testing had been completed by an accredited laboratory. The Declaration of Conformity listed the relevant directives. On a routine documentation audit by a market surveillance authority, it was identified that the product had not been assessed against Articles 3.3(d)(e)(f) of RED - the cybersecurity provisions that became mandatory on 1 August 2025.

The consequence: a voluntary market suspension across all four EU member states while the cybersecurity assessment was completed, existing stock held in warehouse pending resolution, and a revised Declaration of Conformity and updated Technical Construction File required before product could be relisted. Total timeline: eleven weeks from audit notification to relisting. A pre-launch RED cybersecurity gap analysis would have cost a fraction of the commercial and operational disruption.

Common Pain Points

Electronics Compliance Challenges We Solve

These are the situations we encounter most frequently when manufacturers and importers come to TGC - often after a compliance gap has already caused a commercial or regulatory incident.

Declaration of Conformity Missing Applicable Directives

A DoC that was correct at launch may no longer be complete after new requirements are added. RED cybersecurity is the most current example: many existing DoCs for Wi-Fi or Bluetooth products omit Articles 3.3(d)(e)(f) because they were assessed before August 2025. A non-compliant DoC invalidates CE marking for the entire product.

EU and US Compliance Treated as Interchangeable

CE marking and FCC authorisation are entirely separate processes with no mutual recognition. Products with CE marking cannot be placed on the US market without separate FCC authorisation. Products with FCC ID cannot be CE marked on that basis. Conflating the two - or assuming one covers the other - is one of the most common structural errors in electronics market entry planning.

RoHS Exemption Expiry Not Tracked Across Product Portfolio

RoHS exemptions are product-specific and time-limited. Companies with large portfolios frequently lack a system to track which products rely on which exemptions and when those exemptions expire. The non-renewal of Exemptions 6(a) and 6(b) in December 2025 is affecting manufacturers who did not identify their reliance on these exemptions in advance.

No EU Authorised Representative Appointed

Non-EU manufacturers are legally required to appoint an EU Authorised Representative before placing CE-marked products on the EU market. Many exporters discover this requirement only when a distributor or market surveillance authority requests the AR's contact details - at which point the product may already be on-shelf without a legally valid CE marking arrangement.

RED Cybersecurity Gap in Connected Products Launched After Aug 2025

IoT devices, smart home products, and connected wearables that achieved CE marking before August 2025 but are still being manufactured and placed on the EU market now require reassessment against EN 18031. The radio performance and safety testing that formed the basis of the original CE marking does not cover cybersecurity - a separate conformity assessment is required.

WEEE Producer Registration Gaps Across Multiple EU Markets

WEEE producer obligations are administered at member state level - a single EU-wide registration does not exist. Manufacturers selling into multiple EU markets must register in each relevant national WEEE scheme. Many companies are registered in their primary market but not in secondary markets where sales have grown. Failure to register is an enforcement risk and can disrupt distributor relationships when audited.

FAQs

Frequently Asked Questions About Electronics Compliance

Answers to the questions we hear most often from electronics manufacturers, importers, and product teams navigating multi-market compliance.

Electronics sold in the EU typically require CE marking under several directives simultaneously. The core directives for most electronic products are: RoHS Directive (2011/65/EU) - restricts 10 hazardous substances; EMC Directive (2014/30/EU) - electromagnetic compatibility; and Low Voltage Directive (2014/35/EU) for products operating at relevant voltage ranges. Products with radio functionality additionally require compliance with the Radio Equipment Directive (RED, 2014/53/EU).

CE marking requires a completed Declaration of Conformity (DoC) and a Technical Construction File (TCF) maintained by the manufacturer or EU Authorised Representative, both retained for 10 years. There are more than 30 CE marking directives and regulations in total - the specific set that applies depends on the product's functionality, intended use, and physical characteristics. A directive mapping exercise is the correct starting point for any new product or market entry.

From 1 August 2025, all radio equipment placed on the EU market must comply with cybersecurity requirements under Articles 3.3(d), (e), and (f) of RED 2014/53/EU, activated by Delegated Regulation (EU) 2022/30. Article 3.3(d) requires that devices do not harm the network or misuse network resources. Article 3.3(e) requires protection of personal data and user privacy. Article 3.3(f) requires protection against fraud, particularly for payment-capable devices.

Affected products include IoT devices, wearables, connected toys, smart home equipment, baby monitors, and any device capable of connecting to the internet. Compliance is demonstrated through harmonised standards EN 18031-1, EN 18031-2, and EN 18031-3, published in January 2025. Some products require Notified Body involvement in the conformity assessment; others can use self-declaration via Module A. The RED cybersecurity requirements will be superseded by the Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) when it becomes fully applicable on 11 December 2027.

As of 2024, the UK government has indefinitely extended recognition of the CE mark for Great Britain. CE marking is legally equivalent to the UKCA mark for products subject to relevant UK regulations, with no announced end date for this equivalence. Manufacturers can currently place CE-marked products on the GB market without separately obtaining UKCA marking.

However, the underlying UK technical requirements must still be met - in most cases these are identical to EU harmonised standards. Manufacturers planning long-term GB market access should maintain UKCA-compatible technical documentation in case the UK changes its position. Northern Ireland continues to require CE marking under the Windsor Framework, not UKCA. For products sold in both the EU and UK, CE marking currently satisfies both markets.

FCC 47 CFR Part 15 regulates all electronic devices that can emit radiofrequency (RF) energy in the 9 kHz to 3,000 GHz range. Unintentional radiators - devices that generate but do not intentionally transmit RF, such as computers or power supplies - require a Supplier's Declaration of Conformity (SDoC), which involves testing by an accredited laboratory but no pre-approval from the FCC.

Intentional radiators - devices designed to transmit RF, such as Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, or cellular devices - require FCC certification through an FCC-recognised Telecommunications Certification Body (TCB). After certification, the device is assigned an FCC ID that must be permanently affixed to the product. FCC Class B, the more stringent classification, applies to consumer products. The FCC and CE frameworks are entirely separate and must be satisfied independently - there is no mutual recognition between them.

Several key RoHS Annex III exemptions were subject to renewal decisions adopted by the European Commission in September 2025, entering into force on 11 December 2025. Exemption 6(a) — lead as an alloying element in steel for machining purposes and in galvanised steel — was not renewed and expires 12 months after entry into force: by 11 December 2026 for categories 1-7 and 10. Exemption 6(b) — lead as an alloying element in aluminium — was not renewed in its general form: sub-exemption 6(b)-I (recycled scrap aluminium, categories 1-7 and 10) expires 11 December 2026, and 6(b)-II (machining aluminium, categories 1-7 and 10) expires 11 June 2027.

These non-renewals directly affect manufacturers using aluminium alloys in mainstream EEE categories. Applications for renewal of exemptions expiring 31 December 2027 must be submitted by 30 June 2026, as per Article 5(5) of the RoHS Directive (applications cannot be made later than 18 months before expiry). Manufacturers should audit their current reliance on these and other Annex III exemptions now.

Several Asia-Pacific markets require mandatory pre-market certification for electronics. China requires CCC (China Compulsory Certification) for IT equipment, audio/video equipment, and specified electrical products - products cannot be imported, sold, or used in China without CCC. South Korea requires mandatory KC marking for safety and EMC compliance. India requires mandatory BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards) registration for smartphones, laptops, tablets, power banks, and specified peripherals under the Electronics and Information Technology Goods Order.

Japan's VCCI mark is technically voluntary but commercially essential for IT equipment - products without VCCI face significant market access barriers from distributors and retailers. Australia and New Zealand use the mandatory RCM (Regulatory Compliance Mark) for prescribed electrical equipment. South Korea has additionally mandated USB Type-C ports on new consumer devices from 5 November 2026, modelled on EU Directive 2022/2380.
