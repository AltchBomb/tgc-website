Food Supplement Compliance Across Every Market You Export To

Food supplements occupy an unusually complex position in global trade. They sit at the intersection of food law, pharmaceutical regulation, and marketing compliance - and the rules governing which ingredients are permitted, which claims can be made, and how products must be labelled differ substantially between the EU, UK, US, China, and other major markets.

In the EU, over 1,500 botanical health claims have been in a prolonged on-hold state since 2012. On 30 April 2025, the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled that these claims cannot be used in advertising pending Commission authorisation - a ruling with immediate implications for any supplement business marketing products in the EU. In the US, New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) notifications must be submitted to the FDA at least 75 days before marketing - a requirement many foreign supplement manufacturers entering the US market for the first time have not fulfilled. In China, the Blue Hat registration system operates on timelines of two to three years, with eligibility requiring at least one year of commercial sales in the country of manufacture.

Regulatory Landscape 2025-2026

What Governs Food Supplements in Your Target Markets

From the EU's health claims deadlock and US DSHEA pre-market notification requirements to China's dual-track Blue Hat system and the UK's post-Brexit divergence, food supplement regulation is one of the most fragmented areas of global product compliance. Here is the current state by region.

🇪🇺

European Union - Supplements Directive and Health Claims

In force

EU Food Supplements Directive - Directive 2002/46/EC

Directive 2002/46/EC is the core EU regulatory framework for food supplements, harmonising rules on the vitamins and minerals that may be used. Only substances listed in Annexes I and II of the Directive (and its implementing measures) may be used as sources of vitamins and minerals in supplements marketed in the EU. The Directive requires mandatory labelling information including: the term "food supplement"; names of the nutrient or substance categories; recommended daily dose with a warning not to exceed it; and a statement that supplements should not be used as a substitute for a varied diet. Ingredient notification requirements are administered at member state level - most EU countries require pre-market notification, with national competent authorities maintaining their own registers.

Ruling April 2025

CJEU Botanical Health Claims Ruling - Case C-386/23

On 30 April 2025, the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled in Case C-386/23 (Novel Nutriology v Verband Sozialer Wettbewerb) that health claims on botanical substances cannot be advertised without prior Commission authorisation. Over 1,500 botanical health claims have been on hold since 2012 - initially suspended while the Commission sought a revised scientific assessment framework. The CJEU ruling confirmed that this on-hold status does not constitute transitional permission to use the claims. Supplement businesses must review all EU-facing labelling and advertising materials for botanical health claims compliance. The Commission has not published a resolution timeline. Source: CJEU Case C-386/23.

Active

EU Health Claims - Regulation (EC) 1924/2006

Regulation (EC) 1924/2006 governs all nutrition and health claims on foods and food supplements sold in the EU. Health claims must be substantiated by scientific evidence and authorised by the European Commission before use. Authorised claims are published on the EU Register of Authorised Nutrition and Health Claims. The register includes claims for vitamins, minerals, and other non-botanical nutrients. Claims for botanical substances remain on hold following the 2012 suspension and are prohibited under the CJEU April 2025 ruling. Novel food ingredients used in supplements must also be assessed under Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 if they were not consumed to any significant degree in the EU before 15 May 1997.

🇬🇧

United Kingdom - Food Supplements Regulations and Post-Brexit Divergence

In force

UK Food Supplements (England) Regulations 2003

The Food Supplements (England) Regulations 2003 - with equivalent instruments in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland - is the primary UK framework, implementing requirements broadly equivalent to EU Directive 2002/46/EC. Labelling requirements mirror the EU standard: the term "food supplement" must appear on the label; recommended daily dosage and a warning not to exceed it are required; and ingredient forms must be drawn from the permitted list. Unlike most EU member states, the UK does not require pre-market notification before placing a food supplement on the market - products must comply at point of sale, with the Food Standards Agency as the oversight body.

Updated August 2024

UK Vitamin and Mineral Substances List - August 2024 Amendment

The UK amended its permitted vitamins and minerals list with effect from 10 August 2024, under the Food Supplements and Food for Specific Groups (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations. The UK now operates an independent permitted substances schedule rather than automatically mirroring EU Annex updates. This means that ingredient forms permitted under EU Directive 2002/46/EC Annex II may not automatically be permitted in the UK, and vice versa. Supplement businesses operating in both markets must verify ingredient compliance against both the EU Annexes and the UK permitted list separately. The Food Standards Agency guidance on food supplements provides the current UK permitted forms list.

Active monitoring

UK Health Claims - Post-Brexit Register and MHRA Borderline Products

The UK maintains a separate nutrition and health claims register, with independent review pathways managed by the Food Standards Agency. The UK Nutrition and Health Claims Register currently retains the claims that were on the EU authorised list at the point of Brexit. The CJEU ruling on botanical claims (April 2025) does not bind UK courts, but UK law applies equivalent substantiation requirements - botanical claims that are not on the UK authorised register remain prohibited. The MHRA is the borderline authority determining whether a product is classified as a medicine rather than a food supplement, which triggers a wholly different regulatory pathway including a marketing authorisation requirement.

🇺🇸

United States - DSHEA and FDA Regulation

In force

DSHEA 1994 - Dietary Supplements as Food

The Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) 1994 establishes the US regulatory framework for dietary supplements, defining them as food rather than drugs. Under DSHEA, dietary supplements do not require pre-market approval from the FDA - but the manufacturer is responsible for ensuring the product is safe before marketing. The FDA has post-market authority to take action against unsafe or misbranded products. DSHEA covers vitamins, minerals, herbs and botanicals, amino acids, and other dietary ingredients. The US system is therefore fundamentally different from the EU, where a positive list of permitted ingredient forms applies and health claims must be pre-authorised.

Pre-market requirement

New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) Notifications - 75-Day Pre-Market Window

Any dietary ingredient not marketed in the US in a dietary supplement before 15 October 1994 is a New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) and requires a pre-market notification to the FDA at least 75 days before first marketing. The notification must include the ingredient's history of use, safety evidence, and proposed conditions of use. The FDA does not approve NDI notifications but may issue a response letter identifying safety concerns. The FDA maintains a public database of submitted NDI notifications. Updated draft guidance on NDI submissions was issued by the FDA in 2024. Failure to submit a required NDI notification renders the supplement adulterated under US law.

Active enforcement

cGMP Requirements - 21 CFR Part 111 - and Structure/Function Claims

Current Good Manufacturing Practice requirements for dietary supplements are codified at 21 CFR Part 111 and apply to all manufacturers, packagers, labelers, and holders of US dietary supplements - including foreign manufacturers. Key requirements include written quality control procedures, identity and purity testing of components, and batch production records. Structure/function claims (such as "supports immune health") are permitted on dietary supplements without FDA pre-approval, but must be substantiated, must include the required disclaimer ("This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."), and must be notified to the FDA within 30 days of first marketing under 21 CFR 101.93. Disease claims are prohibited and will cause the product to be reclassified as an unapproved drug.

China, Australia and Canada - Key Non-EU/US Markets

2-3 year registration timeline

China - Blue Hat Health Food Registration and Filing

China regulates health food products (保健食品) through a dual-track system administered by SAMR. The registration pathway applies to products making specific health function claims from China's list of 27 approved functions - and requires clinical data, with typical timelines of 2 to 3 years. The filing pathway is available for products using standard approved ingredients (vitamins, minerals, certain functional materials such as CoQ10 and fish oil) and typically takes 6 to 12 months. Both pathways require at least one year of commercial sales in the country of manufacture before the application. Products approved under either pathway carry the Blue Hat logo, which is mandatory for domestic retail and e-commerce channels in China. Cross-border e-commerce sales do not require Blue Hat certification. Source: CIRS Group, China Health Food Registration.

Active

Australia - TGA Complementary Medicines Framework

In Australia, food supplements are regulated as complementary medicines by the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) and must be included in the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG) before sale. The framework offers three pathways: Listed medicines (low-risk, standard ingredients, no pre-market evaluation by TGA, self-certification of quality and safety); Assessed Listed medicines (pre-market TGA review of efficacy evidence); and Registered complementary medicines (full TGA evaluation of quality, safety, and efficacy, required for higher-risk products). The TGA updates its Permissible Ingredients Determination regularly - the March 2024 and June 2024 determinations added new ingredients and revised existing approvals. Products using ingredients outside the permissible list require TGA pre-approval.

Active

Canada - Natural Health Products Regulations

Canada classifies dietary supplements as Natural Health Products (NHPs) under the Natural Health Products Regulations (in force since January 2004), administered by Health Canada's Natural and Non-Prescription Health Products Directorate. Unlike the US, Canada requires pre-market authorisation for NHPs - a Product Licence must be obtained before market entry. Applications must include evidence of safety and efficacy, ingredient specifications, and labelling information. Approved products receive an NPN (Natural Product Number) or DIN-HM (Homeopathic) which must appear on the label. Health Canada proposed amendments to the NHP framework in its 2024-2026 Forward Regulatory Plan, aiming to modernise and streamline the risk-based review process, though specific effective dates for the amendments had not been finalised as of early 2026.

Active Requirements and Key Dates

Food Supplement Compliance: What Is Live Right Now

Unlike some regulatory areas, food supplement compliance does not have a single cluster of future deadlines - the requirements are largely in force now, with a mix of recent changes already active and ongoing obligations that apply at the point of market entry. These are the requirements your supplement business needs to have covered today.

Active

EU - Botanical health claims prohibition (post-CJEU ruling April 2025)

Health claims on botanical substances cannot be advertised on EU-facing labelling or marketing without prior Commission authorisation. Over 1,500 claims remain on hold. Any supplement business currently using on-hold botanical claims in EU markets should review its materials. Source: CJEU Case C-386/23, April 2025.

Active

US - NDI notification required 75 days before marketing new ingredients

Dietary supplement manufacturers must submit a New Dietary Ingredient notification to the FDA at least 75 days before introducing any ingredient first marketed after October 1994. The obligation applies at first marketing - not registration. Source: US FDA, NDI Notification Process.

Aug 2024

UK - Amended permitted vitamins and minerals list in effect

The UK permitted substances list was updated from 10 August 2024, diverging further from EU Annex II. Products containing ingredient forms added to or removed from the UK list require review against current UK rules. Source: Food Standards Agency, Food Supplements.

Active

US - cGMP compliance required for all supplement manufacturers (21 CFR Part 111)

All manufacturers, packagers, labelers, and holders of US dietary supplements - including foreign manufacturers - must comply with 21 CFR Part 111. FDA inspections can result in import alerts and warning letters for non-compliant facilities. Source: eCFR, 21 CFR Part 111.

Active

China - Blue Hat pre-market approval required for domestic retail

Health food products sold through domestic retail, pharmacies, and domestic e-commerce platforms in China must carry Blue Hat certification. Given 2-3 year registration timelines, market entry planning must start well in advance. Source: CIRS Group, China Health Food Registration.

Active

Australia - TGA ARTG listing required before market entry

All complementary medicine products must be entered on the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods before sale. The TGA updates its Permissible Ingredients Determination regularly - the most recent updates were in March and June 2024. Source: TGA, Complementary Medicines.

Why It Matters

The Consequences of Non-Compliance in Food Supplements Are Escalating

Food supplement compliance failures tend to fall into three categories, each with distinct commercial consequences. The first is claims non-compliance - using health claims that are not authorised in the target market. In the EU, the CJEU April 2025 ruling on botanical claims has given well-resourced competitors a direct legal mechanism to challenge non-compliant advertising through unfair competition law, even without regulatory enforcement. In Germany in particular, Abmahnung (cease and desist) procedures from trade associations and competitors are a well-established enforcement channel.

The second is ingredient non-compliance. In the US, failure to file an NDI notification for a post-1994 dietary ingredient does not require FDA action to cause commercial damage - it renders the product adulterated under DSHEA, creating liability for the distributor and giving retailers grounds to delist the product. In the EU and UK, using ingredient forms not on the permitted substances lists carries equivalent consequences, as does introducing a novel food ingredient without the necessary authorisation under Novel Food Regulation (EU) 2015/2283.

The third is market access failure. China's Blue Hat system cannot be bypassed - without registration or filing approval, a product simply cannot be sold through domestic retail channels. Australia's TGA requirement for ARTG listing has the same effect. Businesses that begin market entry planning without accounting for these timelines routinely miss their launch windows by twelve months or more.

Real-World Scenario

Supplement Brand - EU Claims Challenge and US NDI Gap

A UK-based supplement brand launched a botanical sleep support product into Germany and the Netherlands in late 2024, using valerian and lemon balm health claims that had been on the EU on-hold list since 2012. Their position was that the on-hold status implied tolerance - that the claims were permissible while evaluation was pending. Following the CJEU April 2025 ruling, a German trade association issued a cease and desist letter within six weeks, forcing the brand to withdraw its German-market product descriptions and advertising at short notice.

Simultaneously, their US distribution partner flagged that their lead ingredient - a standardised botanical extract introduced to the formulation in 2019 - had no NDI notification on file with the FDA. The product had been sold in the US for over two years. Resolving the NDI gap required a retrospective submission, a 75-day hold on US marketing while the FDA reviewed the notification, and a reformulation review to confirm no other post-1994 ingredients had been overlooked. The combined commercial impact across both markets exceeded $400,000 in lost sales and remediation costs - for compliance issues that a pre-launch regulatory review would have caught in full.

Common Pain Points

Food Supplement Compliance Challenges We Solve

These are the compliance situations we encounter most frequently when supplement businesses come to TGC - often following a costly market disruption that a structured regulatory review would have prevented.

EU Botanical Health Claims - Post-CJEU Ruling Exposure

Many supplement businesses selling into the EU have been using botanical health claims that were placed on hold in 2012, assuming that the on-hold status implied tolerance. The CJEU April 2025 ruling eliminated that assumption - these claims are now explicitly prohibited pending Commission authorisation, and well-resourced competitors can challenge non-compliant advertising through trade association procedures without waiting for regulatory enforcement. EU market labelling and digital marketing materials require immediate review against the authorised claims register.

Missing NDI Notifications for US Market Entry

The NDI notification requirement under DSHEA is routinely missed by foreign supplement manufacturers entering the US market for the first time. Any ingredient first marketed in a US supplement after 15 October 1994 requires a 75-day pre-market notification to the FDA - and the obligation applies to the ingredient in its specific form, concentration, and intended use. A product sold in the US without required NDI notifications is adulterated under US law regardless of its safety profile, creating liability for US distribution partners and giving retailers grounds to delist.

Multi-Jurisdiction Labelling Differences

A supplement label developed for one market rarely satisfies the requirements of another. EU and UK Supplement Facts panels differ from the US format; the EU requires percentages of nutrient reference values (NRVs) while the US uses Daily Values; China requires Chinese-language labels with approved function claim wording; and Australia requires TGA-specific warnings and the ARTG entry number. Businesses entering multiple markets simultaneously with a single label design typically generate compliance gaps in every market they target.

China Blue Hat Timeline Misalignment

Supplement businesses regularly underestimate the China Blue Hat registration timeline - and begin the application process too late to support planned launch dates. Registration typically takes 2 to 3 years and requires one year of commercial sales history in the country of manufacture before the application can be submitted. Filing for standard products takes 6 to 12 months. A product that needs Blue Hat approval and has not yet built its one-year sales history in its home market effectively has a minimum 18-month lead time before it can be sold in Chinese domestic retail - and that assumes no application rejections.

Novel Food Ingredient Status Uncertainty

The novel food status of botanical extracts, concentrated forms of standard ingredients, and newly developed delivery technologies is a persistent source of compliance uncertainty in both the EU and UK. An extract at an unusually high concentration, a botanical used in a novel delivery format, or a probiotic strain with no documented pre-1997 EU consumption history may trigger novel food requirements even when the underlying ingredient is well known. Without a formal novel food status determination, supplement businesses face the risk of enforcement action at the point of sale or a retail delisting triggered by a competitor notification.

UK-EU Regulatory Divergence Post-Brexit

Since Brexit, the UK and EU supplement regulatory frameworks have been diverging progressively. The UK permitted vitamins and minerals list was updated in August 2024 and now operates independently of EU Annex II. UK novel food authorisations and EU novel food authorisations are handled by separate bodies on different timelines. The UK health claims register retains the EU list at the point of Brexit but is not automatically updated. Supplement businesses that treat EU and UK compliance as equivalent are increasingly likely to be wrong - and the gap is widening.

FAQs

Frequently Asked Questions About Food Supplement Compliance

Answers to the questions we hear most often from supplement manufacturers, importers, and brand owners navigating multi-market regulatory requirements.

Directive 2002/46/EC requires that food supplements sold to consumers in the EU carry the term "food supplement" on the label; the names of the nutrient or substance categories characterising the product; the recommended daily dose in a suitable format; a warning not to exceed the recommended daily dose; a statement that food supplements should not be used as a substitute for a varied diet; and a statement that products should be kept out of the reach of young children. The name or address of the manufacturer, packer, or seller established within the EU is required, along with a best-before date and net quantity.

In addition, ingredient forms used must be drawn from those permitted under Annexes I and II of the Directive. Nutrition and health claims on supplements are regulated separately under Regulation (EC) 1924/2006 - only claims on the authorised register may be used, and botanical health claims are currently prohibited in advertising following the CJEU April 2025 ruling. Member states may impose additional notification requirements at national level. Source: EUR-Lex, Directive 2002/46/EC.

On 30 April 2025, the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled in Case C-386/23 (Novel Nutriology v Verband Sozialer Wettbewerb) that health claims on food products containing botanical substances cannot be used in advertising until the European Commission formally authorises them. The ruling arose from a German unfair competition challenge. The Court confirmed that the on-hold status of over 1,500 botanical health claims - maintained since 2012 while the Commission sought a revised scientific framework - does not constitute permission to use those claims. The prohibition applies during the entire period of suspension.

The practical consequence is that supplement businesses marketing products in the EU with botanical health claims on their labels, in digital advertising, or on e-commerce product pages must review and remove non-authorised botanical claims. German trade associations and competitor businesses have a direct legal mechanism to challenge non-compliant advertising through cease and desist procedures under unfair competition law. The Commission has not announced a timeline for resolving the on-hold status. Source: EUR-Lex, CJEU Case C-386/23.

Under DSHEA 1994, a New Dietary Ingredient is any dietary ingredient that was not marketed in a US dietary supplement before 15 October 1994. Manufacturers or distributors who wish to market a dietary supplement containing an NDI must submit a pre-market notification to the FDA at least 75 days before introducing the product to market. The notification must contain the basis on which the manufacturer or distributor has concluded that the NDI or the supplement containing it will reasonably be expected to be safe. The FDA does not approve NDI notifications but may issue a response letter if safety evidence is considered inadequate. Source: US FDA, NDI Notification Process.

Ingredients that were in the food supply in their chemically unaltered form before October 1994 are exempt from the NDI notification requirement. The exemption applies to the specific ingredient form and conditions of use - a novel extract or concentrated form of an otherwise common ingredient may still require an NDI notification. The FDA issued updated draft guidance on NDI submissions in 2024. Failure to submit a required notification renders the supplement adulterated under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, creating legal liability for both the manufacturer and the US importer.

China's health food framework, administered by SAMR, operates on two pathways. The registration pathway is required for products making specific health function claims from China's list of 27 approved functions - it requires clinical data and takes approximately 2 to 3 years. The filing pathway is available for products using only vitamins, minerals, and nutrients from approved directories or approved functional raw materials (such as CoQ10, melatonin, and fish oil) - it takes 6 to 12 months. Both pathways require that the product has been sold commercially in its country of manufacture for at least one year before the application. Source: CIRS Group, China Health Food Registration.

Products approved under either pathway carry the Blue Hat logo, which is mandatory for domestic retail, pharmacy, and domestic e-commerce sales in China. Cross-border e-commerce into China operates under a separate regulatory framework and does not require Blue Hat certification - it is commonly used as an interim market entry route while a domestic registration is in progress. Approved registrations have a five-year validity period and must be renewed before expiry.

The UK and EU food supplement frameworks have been diverging since Brexit. The most significant recent change is the UK permitted vitamins and minerals list, updated in August 2024 to operate independently of EU Annex II - meaning that ingredient forms permitted in the EU may not automatically be permitted in the UK. The UK Food Standards Agency does not require pre-market notification before placing a supplement on the UK market, unlike most EU member states, which operate national notification systems with their own competent authorities. Source: Food Standards Agency, Food Supplements Guidance.

The UK maintains a separate nutrition and health claims register. The CJEU April 2025 ruling on botanical claims does not bind UK courts, but UK law applies equivalent substantiation requirements - botanical claims that are not on the UK authorised register are not permitted. The MHRA continues to act as the borderline authority between supplements and medicines in the UK, applying the medicinal product definition under UK law. Businesses operating in both EU and UK markets must maintain parallel regulatory assessments, as equivalent does not mean identical.

Current Good Manufacturing Practice requirements for dietary supplements in the US are codified at 21 CFR Part 111. These regulations apply to all manufacturers, packagers, labelers, and holders of US dietary supplements - including foreign manufacturers whose products are imported. Key requirements include: written quality control procedures; component identity and purity testing before production use; batch production records; controls to prevent contamination, mix-ups, and errors; and sample retention from each batch.

The regulations require that dietary supplements are manufactured consistently and meet established specifications for identity, purity, strength, and composition. FDA inspections of supplement facilities assess cGMP compliance - violations can result in warning letters, consent decrees, product seizures, or import alerts that prevent a manufacturer's products from entering the US. Foreign manufacturers are subject to the same requirements as domestic manufacturers and may be inspected by the FDA at their overseas facilities. Maintaining a current cGMP-compliant quality system is a prerequisite for sustainable US market access.
