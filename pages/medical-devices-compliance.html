What We Handle

Medical Device Compliance Across Every Major Market You Target

Medical devices occupy one of the most tightly regulated product sectors in global trade. The EU, UK, US, and China each maintain independent conformity assessment frameworks with distinct classification systems, pre-market pathway requirements, quality management standards, and post-market surveillance obligations - and none of them directly recognises the others.

In the EU, EU MDR 2017/745 has been fully applicable since 26 May 2021, requiring all non-EU manufacturers to appoint an EU Authorised Representative under Article 13, and imposing significantly stricter Notified Body oversight, technical documentation, and post-market surveillance requirements than the MDD it replaced. Legacy devices operating under transitional provisions face hard deadlines of 31 December 2027 (Class III and IIb implantables) and 31 December 2028 (Class IIb other, IIa, and Class I with Notified Body involvement) under Regulation (EU) 2023/607. In parallel, EUDAMED becomes mandatory from 28 May 2026.

In the US, the FDA cleared approximately 3,000 devices per year via the 510(k) pathway, while the Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR) - which revised 21 CFR Part 820 to incorporate ISO 13485:2016 by reference - became effective 2 February 2026, replacing the legacy Quality System Regulation inspection framework. In the UK, CE marking for MDR/IVDR-compliant devices remains valid until 30 June 2030 under current MHRA transitional arrangements, with the MHRA consulting on indefinite recognition as of February 2026. In China, all imported devices require national NMPA registration under State Council Order 739, with a mandatory local agent requirement.

TGC provides specialist medical device compliance support for manufacturers, importers, and distributors operating across multiple markets. We combine deep regulatory expertise with practical device experience - covering EU Authorised Representative provision, CE marking documentation and Notified Body coordination, EUDAMED registration, FDA 510(k) submission support, UK Responsible Person services, China NMPA local agent representation, and ongoing multi-market post-market surveillance programme management.

European Union - MDR 2017/745 and IVDR 2017/746

In force from 26 May 2021

EU Medical Devices Regulation - Regulation (EU) 2017/745

Regulation (EU) 2017/745 became fully applicable on 26 May 2021, replacing the Medical Devices Directive 93/42/EEC and the Active Implantable Medical Devices Directive 90/385/EEC. The MDR introduces significantly stronger requirements across the device lifecycle: a mandatory EU Authorised Representative for all non-EU manufacturers (Article 13); stricter Notified Body oversight for Class IIa, IIb, and III devices; 22 classification rules in Annex VIII covering four risk classes (I, IIa, IIb, III); comprehensive technical documentation requirements including clinical evaluation reports; and enhanced post-market surveillance obligations including periodic safety update reports and post-market clinical follow-up. Class I non-sterile, non-measuring devices may still be self-certified, but all other classes require Notified Body involvement and CE marking. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2017/745.

Deadlines: Dec 2027 and Dec 2028

MDR Transitional Provisions - Regulation (EU) 2023/607

Regulation (EU) 2023/607 extended the Article 120 transitional arrangements, allowing legacy MDD/AIMDD-certified devices to remain on the EU market under specified conditions. The final deadlines are 31 December 2027 for Class III and Class IIb implantable devices, and 31 December 2028 for Class IIb (other than implantables), Class IIa, and Class I devices requiring Notified Body involvement (sterile or measuring function). To benefit from these extensions, manufacturers must have had no significant design or intended purpose changes, and a signed written agreement with a Notified Body must have been in place. Manufacturers without a compliant MDR certificate by the applicable deadline will lose EU market access. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2023/607.

Mandatory from 28 May 2026

EUDAMED - Commission Decision (EU) 2025/2371

Commission Decision (EU) 2025/2371, adopted 26 November 2025, sets 28 May 2026 as the mandatory start date for EUDAMED's UDI and device registration module, along with three other modules (economic operators, certificates and declarations of conformity, and market surveillance). From this date, all new medical devices must be registered in EUDAMED before EU market placement, with valid UDI assigned. Devices already on the market have until 28 November 2026 to complete registration. EU Authorised Representatives are responsible for EUDAMED registration on behalf of non-EU manufacturers. Source: EUR-Lex, Commission Decision (EU) 2025/2371.

IVDR transition in progress

EU IVDR 2017/746 - Staggered Transition to 2029

Regulation (EU) 2017/746 on in vitro diagnostic medical devices became applicable on 26 May 2022. Legacy IVDD-certified IVDs benefit from extended transitional periods under Regulation (EU) 2024/1860: Class D IVDs must be IVDR-compliant by December 2027; Class C by December 2028; and Class B and Class A sterile IVDs by December 2029. The IVDR establishes a new four-class system (A through D), with D representing the highest risk (blood grouping, transfusion safety). Notified Body involvement is mandatory for all Class B, C, and D IVDs. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2017/746.

United Kingdom - MHRA and Transitional CE Recognition

In force

UK Medical Devices Regulations 2002 (SI 2002/618)

The UK Medical Devices Regulations 2002 (as amended post-Brexit) remains the primary framework for medical devices placed on the Great Britain market. All medical devices - including Class I, IIa, IIb, III, and IVDs - must be registered with the MHRA before being placed on the GB market. Non-UK manufacturers must appoint a UK Responsible Person to act on their behalf in dealings with the MHRA. Northern Ireland, under the Windsor Framework, continues to follow EU MDR and IVDR requirements and requires EU MDR CE marking for market access. Source: MHRA, Regulating Medical Devices in the UK.

Active - valid until 2028/2030

CE Marking Transitional Acceptance for Medical Devices

CE-marked devices compliant with the EU MDR or EU IVDR may be placed on the Great Britain market until 30 June 2030. Devices CE-marked under the legacy EU Medical Devices Directive (MDD) or AIMDD may remain on the GB market until the certificate expires or until 30 June 2028, whichever is earlier. These transitional deadlines apply to Great Britain only - Northern Ireland follows EU rules. UKCA marking for medical devices has not been mandated and CE marking remains the practical route for most manufacturers. Source: MHRA, UK Medical Devices Guidance.

Consultation open Feb 2026

MHRA Proposal to Recognise CE Marking Indefinitely

On 16 February 2026, the MHRA launched a public consultation proposing to recognise CE-marked medical devices indefinitely in Great Britain, removing the 2028 and 2030 sunset deadlines currently in place. The consultation runs until 10 April 2026. If confirmed, this would align Great Britain's medical device market access route permanently with EU MDR and IVDR conformity, significantly reducing the regulatory burden for manufacturers operating in both markets. Manufacturers should monitor the outcome of this consultation before making UKCA marking investment decisions. Source: UK Government, MHRA Consultation, February 2026.

Active - June 2025 implementation

Post-Market Surveillance Requirements - 2024 Amendment

The Medical Devices (Post-market Surveillance Requirements) (Amendment) (Great Britain) Regulations 2024 came into force on 16 June 2025, introducing mandatory post-market surveillance plans, periodic safety update reports, and reduced incident reporting deadlines for devices on the Great Britain market. These requirements align with the enhanced PMS obligations under EU MDR/IVDR, reducing divergence between the GB and EU frameworks. Device manufacturers must ensure their existing post-market programmes have been updated to meet the new GB standards. Source: UK Government, Future Medical Device Regulation Implementation.

🇺🇸

United States - FDA CDRH, 510(k), PMA, and QMSR

In force

FDA Device Classification and Premarket Pathways

The US FDA regulates medical devices under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, as substantially amended by the Medical Device Amendments of 1976 which established the current classification and premarket notification framework. Devices are assigned to one of three classes: Class I (approximately 47% of all FDA-regulated devices, generally exempt from premarket notification and subject to general controls only); Class II (approximately 43%, typically requiring a 510(k) premarket notification demonstrating substantial equivalence to a legally marketed predicate device); and Class III (approximately 10%, requiring Premarket Approval (PMA) based on valid scientific evidence including clinical data). The FDA clears approximately 3,000 devices per year via the 510(k) pathway. All establishments manufacturing or importing medical devices must register with the FDA under 21 CFR Part 807 and list their devices annually. Source: FDA, Classify Your Medical Device.

Effective 2 February 2026

FDA Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR) - Revised 21 CFR Part 820

The FDA's final rule revising 21 CFR Part 820 - now the Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR) - was published 2 February 2024 and became effective 2 February 2026. The QMSR incorporates ISO 13485:2016 by reference, harmonising US quality system requirements with the international standard for the first time. The QSIT (Quality System Inspection Technique) inspection approach was withdrawn on the effective date and replaced with updated procedures under Compliance Program 7382.850. All registered device manufacturers must operate an ISO 13485:2016-aligned QMS, and FDA inspectors now reference the ISO 13485 requirements directly when assessing manufacturer quality systems. Foreign manufacturers supplying the US are equally subject to QMSR requirements. Source: Federal Register, QMSR Final Rule, 2 February 2024.

Available pathway

De Novo Classification - Novel Device Pathway

The De Novo classification request provides a regulatory pathway for novel low-to-moderate risk devices that lack a suitable predicate for 510(k) and would otherwise be automatically classified as Class III. A manufacturer may submit a De Novo request directly (without a prior 510(k) rejection) under the amended pathway introduced by the FDA Safety and Innovation Act (FDASIA) of 2012. Devices granted De Novo classification are typically designated Class I or Class II, establishing a new device type that can serve as a predicate for future 510(k) submissions. De Novo clearance is significant for innovative device manufacturers introducing a new category of technology to the US market. Source: FDA, De Novo Classification Request.

China - NMPA Order 739 and National Registration

In force from 1 June 2021

Regulations on the Supervision and Administration of Medical Devices - Order 739

China's current medical device framework is governed by the Regulations on the Supervision and Administration of Medical Devices (State Council Order 739), effective 1 June 2021. Devices are classified into three risk classes: Class I (low risk) requires filing at city-level market supervision authority; Class II (medium risk) requires registration at provincial NMPA level; and Class III (high risk, including life-supporting and implantable devices) requires national NMPA registration. Crucially, all imported medical devices - regardless of class - require national NMPA registration, meaning foreign manufacturers do not benefit from the city or provincial pathways available for domestically-manufactured Class I and II products. Source: NMPA, Order 739 Implementation Notice.

Mandatory requirement

China Local Agent Requirement for Foreign Manufacturers

All foreign manufacturers importing medical devices into China must appoint a single designated China agent. The agent's name, address, and contact details appear on the NMPA registration certificate and must be included on all product labelling and instructions for use. The agent is responsible for regulatory communications with the NMPA, maintenance of the registration certificate, post-market surveillance and adverse event reporting, and renewal of registrations before the five-year validity expires. Each imported medical device registration may list only one China agent - the single-agent rule requires careful planning in multi-distributor supply chains. Source: NMPA, Medical Device Registration and Filing Provisions 2024.

Quality system requirement

ISO 13485 and GB/YY Standards Compliance

Medical device manufacturers supplying China must demonstrate compliance with ISO 13485 quality management system requirements (reflected in the Chinese equivalent YY/T 0287) and with applicable GB and YY technical standards. Clinical evaluation is required for all imported Class II and Class III devices. Clinical trials conducted in China may be required for Class III devices involving novel technology, and the technical evaluation is conducted by the Centre for Medical Device Evaluation (CMDE) on behalf of the NMPA. In January 2025, NMPA published updated draft manufacturing quality management standards further aligning with ISO 13485:2016. Registration certificates are valid for five years and must be renewed before expiry to avoid loss of market access. Source: NMPA Official Site.

Medical device compliance in 2025-2026 is defined by a convergence of live deadlines that affect both legacy devices and new market entrants. EU MDR transition deadlines are advancing, EUDAMED becomes mandatory in May 2026, and the FDA's revised quality system regulation is already in force. These are the requirements that need to be on every device manufacturer's compliance roadmap today.

US - FDA QMSR effective: ISO 13485:2016 incorporated into 21 CFR Part 820

The FDA's Quality Management System Regulation became effective on 2 February 2026. All registered US device establishments must now operate a QMS aligned with ISO 13485:2016. FDA inspectors assess quality systems against ISO 13485 requirements directly. Source: FDA, QMSR.

All new medical devices placed on the EU market from 28 May 2026 must be registered in EUDAMED with a valid UDI before market placement. Devices already on market have until 28 November 2026 to complete registration. EU Authorised Representatives are responsible for EUDAMED registration. Source: Commission Decision (EU) 2025/2371.

30 Jun 2028

UK - Legacy MDD/AIMDD CE-marked devices: deadline for GB market access

Medical devices CE-marked under the legacy EU MDD or AIMDD directives may remain on the Great Britain market only until 30 June 2028 (or until the certificate expires, whichever is earlier). Manufacturers relying on legacy MDD CE marks must plan their MHRA registration and transition to MDR/IVDR-compliant conformity assessment. Source: MHRA, UK Medical Device Guidance.

31 Dec 2027

EU - MDR transition deadline for Class III and Class IIb implantable devices

Legacy MDD/AIMDD-certified Class III and Class IIb implantable devices may remain on the EU market under transitional provisions only until 31 December 2027, subject to compliance with the conditions of Regulation (EU) 2023/607. A valid MDR Notified Body certificate is required for continued EU sales after this date. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2023/607.

31 Dec 2027

EU - IVDR transition deadline for legacy Class D IVDs

Legacy IVDD-certified Class D IVDs (highest risk - blood grouping, transfusion safety) must be IVDR-compliant by December 2027 under Regulation (EU) 2024/1860. Notified Body engagement and technical documentation upgrades should already be in progress. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2024/1860.

31 Dec 2028

EU - MDR transition deadline for Class IIb (other), Class IIa, and Class I NB devices

The final MDR transitional deadline under Regulation (EU) 2023/607 applies to Class IIb (non-implantable), Class IIa, and Class I devices requiring Notified Body involvement. After 31 December 2028, all such devices must hold valid MDR Notified Body certificates for continued EU market access. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2023/607.

30 Jun 2030

UK - MDR/IVDR CE-marked devices: current deadline for GB market access

Medical devices CE-marked under EU MDR 2017/745 or EU IVDR 2017/746 are currently accepted on the Great Britain market until 30 June 2030, subject to MHRA registration and UK Responsible Person appointment. The MHRA consultation on indefinite recognition (open until April 2026) may remove this deadline. Source: MHRA, UK Medical Device Guidance.

Why It Matters

Medical Device Compliance Failures Have Direct Commercial and Legal Consequences

The consequences of medical device non-compliance are not limited to regulatory fines. In the EU, a device placed on the market without a valid CE mark under EU MDR, or without a registered EU Authorised Representative, is subject to immediate market withdrawal by national competent authorities and may be referred to the EU Safety Gate rapid alert system - generating reputational damage that outlasts the remediation process itself.

In the US, placing a device on the market without the required 510(k) clearance or PMA constitutes introduction of an adulterated or misbranded device under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act. The FDA may issue a Warning Letter, seize products, seek an injunction, or impose import alerts that prevent the manufacturer from shipping any products into the US. Import alerts are publicly listed and routinely reviewed by US distributors, procurement teams, and hospital supply chains as part of supplier qualification.

In China, the single-agent requirement creates a structural commercial dependency: the China agent's name is registered on the NMPA certificate. If the agent relationship breaks down, a replacement agent cannot be appointed without a certificate amendment process. Device manufacturers who have not structured their China market entry arrangements to address agent succession risk face the prospect of certificate suspension - and loss of China market access - at a critical business transition point.

Real-World Scenario

Medical Device Manufacturer - EU Authorised Representative Gap and US QMSR Readiness

A South Korean manufacturer of Class IIa surgical instruments had been selling in the EU through a distribution agreement that included an informal CE marking arrangement with their German distributor. Following a market surveillance inquiry by the German Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices (BfArM) in late 2025, it emerged that no formal EU Authorised Representative had been registered in EUDAMED as required under MDR Article 13 - and the existing MDD certificate had not been transitioned to an MDR Notified Body certificate. BfArM initiated a formal non-conformity procedure, requiring immediate cessation of EU sales pending remediation.

Simultaneously, the same manufacturer was preparing a 510(k) submission to enter the US market. Their quality management system documentation had been prepared against the legacy 21 CFR Part 820 Quality System Regulation - not the revised QMSR that incorporated ISO 13485:2016 by reference from 2 February 2026. Their US regulatory counsel identified that FDA inspectors reviewing their establishment would now reference ISO 13485 clauses directly, requiring a QMS gap assessment and update before the inspection. The combined effect was a six-month delay to EU market restoration and a four-month delay to US 510(k) submission - both arising from compliance gaps that a structured multi-market regulatory review would have identified in advance.

Common Pain Points

Medical Device Compliance Challenges We Solve

These are the compliance situations we encounter most frequently when device manufacturers come to TGC - often following a market access disruption, a regulatory inquiry, or a failed product launch that a structured regulatory review would have prevented.

No EU Authorised Representative Under MDR Article 13

The MDR's Authorised Representative requirement is one of the most frequently overlooked compliance obligations for non-EU device manufacturers. Under EU MDR Article 13, every non-EU manufacturer placing a device on the EU market must appoint a single EU-based Authorised Representative who is jointly liable for MDR compliance. Devices placed on the EU market without a registered Authorised Representative are non-compliant and subject to market withdrawal. National market surveillance authorities actively check EUDAMED registration for non-EU manufacturer representation - particularly following the EUDAMED mandatory launch in May 2026.

MDR Transition Certificate Gaps

Many device manufacturers assumed that extended MDR transitional provisions gave them extended time to avoid Notified Body engagement - only to discover that the transitional extensions require a signed written agreement with a Notified Body as a condition of the grace period. Manufacturers who have not secured Notified Body agreements and are not actively progressing MDR conformity assessment now face the real prospect of losing EU market access at the December 2027 or December 2028 deadlines with insufficient time to complete certification. IVDR transition timelines for IVD manufacturers present an equivalent risk.

510(k) Predicate Strategy and QMSR Readiness

US 510(k) submissions frequently fail or are placed on hold because the predicate device selection strategy has not been adequately developed - and the substantial equivalence argumentation does not survive FDA reviewer scrutiny. Separately, the FDA's QMSR effective from 2 February 2026 has changed the quality system inspection framework: manufacturers whose QMS documentation was built against the legacy QSR format now face inspection against ISO 13485 clause requirements. Manufacturers entering the US market for the first time must ensure both their 510(k) strategy and their quality system are aligned with the current regulatory framework.

China Agent Risk and Registration Continuity

The NMPA's single-agent requirement means that a Chinese local agent is embedded in the registration certificate and cannot be changed without a formal amendment process. Device manufacturers who have appointed an agent through their primary China distributor face a structural conflict: if the distribution relationship ends, the registered agent changes and market access may be interrupted during the amendment process. Structuring the China agent arrangement to be independent of distribution agreements - and understanding the certificate amendment timeline - is critical for manufacturers with substantial China revenue.

EUDAMED Registration and UDI Assignment

EUDAMED registration requires a valid UDI for each device, correct classification under EU MDR/IVDR, an active Authorised Representative, and accurate registration of all economic operators in the supply chain. Manufacturers with large product portfolios frequently discover data quality issues, missing UDI assignments, or incorrect device classifications during EUDAMED pre-registration review. With the mandatory EUDAMED launch on 28 May 2026, manufacturers that have not completed their UDI assignment and EUDAMED data preparation will be unable to legally place new devices on the EU market from that date.

Multi-Market Compliance Programme Management

Device manufacturers targeting EU, UK, US, and China simultaneously must manage four independent conformity assessment timelines, four different quality system inspection regimes, four separate representative or agent relationships, and four distinct post-market surveillance reporting frameworks - with different nomenclature, documentation formats, and regulatory calendars in each market. Without a coordinated multi-market compliance programme, manufacturers routinely prioritise one market's requirements at the expense of another, generating compliance gaps that accumulate until a market access event forces a reactive remediation effort.

Regulation (EU) 2023/607 extended the MDR Article 120 transitional provisions for legacy devices CE-marked under the MDD or AIMDD. To benefit, the device must not have undergone significant changes in design or intended purpose, and a signed written agreement with a Notified Body must be in place. The transition deadlines are: 31 December 2027 for Class III and Class IIb implantable devices; and 31 December 2028 for Class IIb (other than implantables), Class IIa, and Class I devices requiring Notified Body involvement. After the applicable deadline, a valid MDR Notified Body certificate is required for continued EU market access.

Separately, the IVDR transitional periods under Regulation (EU) 2024/1860 set compliance deadlines of December 2027 for Class D legacy IVDs, December 2028 for Class C, and December 2029 for Class B and Class A sterile IVDs. Manufacturers with large IVD portfolios subject to IVDR should already have Notified Body engagements underway for higher-risk classes. Source: EUR-Lex, Regulation (EU) 2023/607.

Most Class II medical devices require a 510(k) premarket notification demonstrating substantial equivalence to a legally marketed predicate device. The 510(k) pathway was established by the Medical Device Amendments of 1976 and requires the submitter to show that the device has the same intended use and equivalent - or different but not raising new safety questions about - technological characteristics. The FDA clears approximately 3,000 devices per year through this pathway.

Class I devices (approximately 47% of all FDA-regulated devices) are generally exempt from premarket notification and subject only to general controls. Class III devices require Premarket Approval (PMA), involving clinical data, scientific review, and manufacturing facility inspection. Novel devices without a suitable predicate may pursue De Novo classification, which can result in Class I or Class II designation and establish a new predicate. All registered establishments must also comply with the QMSR (21 CFR Part 820), which became effective 2 February 2026 and incorporates ISO 13485:2016 by reference. Source: FDA, Classify Your Medical Device.

CE marking remains the primary route to the Great Britain medical device market through extended MHRA transitional arrangements. Devices CE-marked under the EU MDR or IVDR may be placed on the Great Britain market until 30 June 2030. Devices CE-marked under the legacy EU MDD or AIMDD may remain until the certificate expires or until 30 June 2028, whichever is earlier. All devices on the GB market must be registered with the MHRA, and non-UK manufacturers must appoint a UK Responsible Person. Source: MHRA, Regulating Medical Devices in the UK.

On 16 February 2026, the MHRA launched a consultation proposing to recognise CE-marked medical devices indefinitely in Great Britain - potentially removing the 2028 and 2030 deadlines entirely. The consultation runs until 10 April 2026. Northern Ireland continues to follow EU MDR and IVDR requirements under the Windsor Framework, meaning EU MDR CE marking is required for Northern Ireland market access regardless of the GB transitional position. Manufacturers should not assume that GB and Northern Ireland compliance requirements are equivalent. Source: MHRA Consultation, February 2026.

China's medical device framework is governed by State Council Order 739, effective 1 June 2021. Devices are classified into three risk classes, but all imported medical devices - regardless of class - require national NMPA registration. Foreign manufacturers must appoint a single designated China agent, whose name and address appear on the NMPA registration certificate and on all product labelling and instructions for use. The agent manages regulatory communications, post-market surveillance reporting, and certificate renewal. Registration certificates are valid for five years.

Quality management systems must comply with ISO 13485 (reflected in YY/T 0287) and devices must be tested in NMPA-designated laboratories against applicable GB and YY technical standards. Clinical evaluation is required for all imported Class II and Class III devices. For Class III devices involving novel technology, locally conducted clinical trials may be required. The CMDE (Centre for Medical Device Evaluation) conducts technical reviews on behalf of the NMPA. Source: NMPA, Medical Device Registration and Filing Provisions 2024.

EUDAMED is the European Database on Medical Devices, comprising six modules covering UDI and device registration, economic operators, certificates and declarations of conformity, clinical investigations, vigilance, and market surveillance. Commission Decision (EU) 2025/2371, adopted 26 November 2025, set 28 May 2026 as the mandatory date for the first four modules - including the UDI and device module. From this date, all new medical devices placed on the EU market must be registered in EUDAMED with a valid UDI before market placement. Devices already on the market before 28 May 2026 have until 28 November 2026 to complete registration.

EU Authorised Representatives are responsible for EUDAMED registration on behalf of non-EU manufacturers. Registration requires a valid UDI-DI (Device Identifier) assigned by an FDA-accredited issuing agency (such as GS1, HIBCC, or ICCBBA). Manufacturers should begin UDI assignment and EUDAMED data preparation well in advance of the May 2026 deadline - particularly if they have large device portfolios requiring individual device registration. Devices without EUDAMED registration after the mandatory date cannot lawfully be placed on the EU market. Source: European Commission, EUDAMED Overview.
