Chemical Compliance Across the Full Product Lifecycle

Chemical compliance is not a one-time registration exercise. It is an ongoing obligation that spans substance classification, safety documentation, labelling, supply chain disclosure, and market-specific registration - in every country where your products are manufactured, imported, or sold.

Global regulators including ECHA, the US EPA, and the UK's Health and Safety Executive are intensifying oversight. A comprehensive REACH revision (REACH 2.0) is under active development - the European Commission published its proposal for consultation in April 2025, but the final adoption timeline has slipped into 2026 or later following a negative opinion from the EU Regulatory Scrutiny Board (RSB) in September 2025, which requires the impact assessment to be revised before the legislative proposal can proceed. Industry opposition - notably from Cefic and other industry bodies - has added further pressure. PFAS restrictions are expanding across the EU, US, UK, and APAC simultaneously. And enforcement actions are increasing across all major markets.

TGC's chemical compliance team provides the regulatory intelligence, documentation support, and expert guidance to keep your substances, mixtures, and finished goods market-ready - without dedicating headcount to tracking a regulatory landscape that changes quarterly.

Regulatory Landscape 2025 - 2026

What's Changing in Chemical Compliance

The regulatory burden on chemical manufacturers and importers is accelerating. Here is what you need to know by region, updated to reflect the current state of global chemical law.

🇺🇸

United States - TSCA

Deadline 2026

TSCA PFAS Reporting Rule - Submission Window Opens 2026

Under TSCA Section 8(a)(7), any entity that manufactured or imported PFAS between 2011 and 2022 must submit detailed reports to the EPA covering uses, volumes, disposal, and health effects. The submission window now runs from April 13 to October 13, 2026 for most manufacturers. In November 2025, the EPA proposed six exemptions - including a 0.1% de minimis threshold and an imported articles exemption - that could reduce the reporting universe from 131,000 to approximately 4,000 businesses. The final rule is expected mid-2026. Companies should assess their exposure now, since the compressed three-month submission window leaves no room for late preparation.

Active enforcement

TSCA TCE Rule - Enforcement Delayed to May 2026

The EPA's final rule prohibiting most uses of trichloroethylene (TCE) has been subject to multiple enforcement postponements. As of February 2026, the effective date for TSCA Section 6(g) exemption conditions was further pushed to May 18, 2026. Companies relying on TCE exemptions must ensure compliance documentation is current ahead of this date.

Import scrutiny

Expanded Import Enforcement

In December 2025, the EPA announced a major expansion of import investigative capacity, targeting illegal chemical imports with particular focus on Chinese supply chains. Importers should expect increased CBP scrutiny and should ensure TSCA chemical inventory compliance and PFAS disclosure documentation is in order.

🇪🇺

European Union - REACH 2.0 & CLP

Major revision

REACH 2.0 - Comprehensive Revision in Progress

The European Commission published its REACH revision proposal for consultation in April 2025. The final adoption timeline has slipped into 2026 or later - primarily because the EU Regulatory Scrutiny Board (RSB) issued a negative opinion on the impact assessment in September 2025, requiring the Commission to revise and resubmit before the legislative proposal can proceed. Industry opposition from Cefic, EFCC, and IFRA at the April 2025 CARACAL meeting has added additional pressure. The direction of travel is clear regardless of timing: 10-year registration validity periods, ECHA revocation powers for incomplete dossiers, stronger controls on endocrine disruptors and PMT/vPvM substances, polymer registration for up to 30,000 high-concern polymers, and new requirements for distance sales operators. Companies selling into the EU who use an Only Representative (OR) should review their arrangements now regardless of final timing.

2026 deadline

PFAS Annex XVII Restriction - In Progress, Decision Expected 2027+

A major REACH Annex XVII restriction on PFAS - covering firefighting foams and multiple industrial sectors - is under active development but has not yet entered force. ECHA's scientific committees (RAC and SEAC) are finalising their evaluations, with SEAC's draft opinion consultation expected to open after the March 2026 committee meeting. A final European Commission decision is not expected before 2027, with implementation potentially 2028 - 2029. Companies in electronics, textiles, automotive, and consumer goods should begin PFAS supply chain mapping now - not to meet a 2026 deadline, but to be positioned well ahead of what will be a significant restriction when adopted.

Ongoing

SVHC Candidate List - Now 251+ Substances

ECHA's SVHC Candidate List reached 251 substances in 2025, with additions continuing. Substances above 0.1% w/w in articles trigger mandatory disclosure obligations to customers and, on request, to consumers within 45 days. All SVHC updates require immediate SDS and label review.

🇬🇧

United Kingdom - UK REACH

Extended deadlines

UK REACH Registration - Extended to 2026 - 2030

Following Brexit, the UK established its independent chemical regulation under the Health and Safety Executive (HSE). While UK REACH mirrors many EU REACH requirements, it operates as a separate legal framework. Registration deadlines have been extended in phases from 2026 to 2030 depending on tonnage and hazard level. Manufacturers and importers placing chemicals on the Great Britain market must register separately - EU REACH registration provides no automatic right to sell in GB.

Divergence risk

UK SVHC Candidate List - Diverging from EU

The UK's SVHC list is managed independently and is beginning to diverge from the EU's Candidate List. Companies operating in both markets need separate monitoring processes to ensure disclosure and SDS obligations are met in each jurisdiction. The risk of inadvertent non-compliance increases with every UK-specific update.

🌏

Asia-Pacific - China, Korea, Japan

Major reform

K-REACH Korea - 2025 Structural Amendments

Korea's K-REACH underwent significant structural reform effective January 2025, raising annual tonnage thresholds for new substance registration and introducing a new "unconfirmed hazardous substances" category. From August 7, 2025, the former "toxic substances" framework under the Chemical Control Act was replaced with a more detailed human and environmental hazard classification. Companies exporting to Korea must update SDS documentation - old MSDS formats are only valid until June 30, 2026; new formats are mandatory from July 1, 2026.

Active

China MEE Order 12 - New Substance Registration

Under MEE Order 12, all new chemical substances must be registered with China's Ministry of Ecology and Environment before manufacture or import. Enforcement has intensified, with non-compliant imports subject to detention and market bans. Simplified registration routes are available for low-risk substances; standard registration requires detailed hazard and exposure data.

April 2025

Japan ISHL Update - GHS SDS Mandatory

Japan's Industrial Safety and Health Law (ISHL) April 2025 update enforces GHS-based Safety Data Sheets and label standards for hundreds of chemical substances. Government-led data collection projects now require submission of verifiable test data. Companies exporting chemical products to Japan should verify SDS compliance against the updated Japanese GHS format.

Why It Matters

The Cost of Chemical Non-Compliance Is Significant - and Rising

Chemical non-compliance carries consequences across multiple dimensions simultaneously. A single documentation gap or classification error can trigger regulatory penalties, customs holds, mandatory product recalls, and civil liability - often at the same time.

Under TSCA, civil penalties can exceed $48,500 per day per violation - the statutory base of $37,500 set by the 2016 Lautenberg Act has been inflation-adjusted annually under 40 CFR § 19, reaching over $48,500 as of the 2024/2025 adjustment. Under EU REACH, failure to register a substance above the 1 tonne-per-year threshold can result in import bans and forced product withdrawal. Under K-REACH in Korea, updated from July 2026, operating with out-of-date SDS documentation becomes a direct compliance violation.

The pattern we see across chemical compliance failures is consistent: the regulatory horizon is known well in advance, but internal teams lack the specialist bandwidth to track it. By the time a gap is identified - often during a port inspection, a customer audit, or a regulatory notification - the cost of remediation far exceeds the cost of proactive compliance.

Real-World Scenario

Cleaning Product Shipment - EU Market Entry Blocked

A company shipping industrial cleaning agents to Europe had its consignment blocked at port. The products contained trace levels of formaldehyde - a substance regulated under REACH's Annex XVII and CLP's CMR classification rules. The combination of inadequate CLP labelling, missing SDS documentation aligned to the EU's GHS implementation, and no registered Only Representative resulted in the shipment being detained for six weeks.

The direct cost: warehouse fees, re-testing, label redesign, legal counsel, and lost retail contracts. Total estimated impact exceeded €200,000 - for a gap that an SVHC review and SDS audit would have identified in advance.

Challenges We Solve

Common Chemical Compliance Challenges

These are the compliance gaps we see most frequently when businesses come to TGC - often after a costly incident that a proactive review would have prevented.

SVHC Cascade - Supplier Data Gaps

Your product contains an SVHC above 0.1% w/w but your supplier hasn't disclosed it. You are now in breach of REACH Article 33 disclosure obligations without knowing it. TGC's supplier engagement process resolves this before it becomes a customer or regulator complaint.

SDS Accuracy - Outdated or Wrong-Format Documents

SDSs authored years ago often contain classification errors, missing hazard categories, or wrong GHS pictograms against current CLP Annex VI harmonised classifications. A single customer audit or customs inspection can surface this immediately. We audit and correct entire SDS libraries.

Multi-Market Label Divergence

CLP (EU), GHS (global), OSHA HazCom (US), and WHMIS (Canada) share a GHS foundation but diverge on pictogram requirements, signal words, and language mandates. A single label that works in the EU may be non-compliant in the UK post-Brexit or in Korea. We manage market-specific label sets.

REACH Registration - New Market Entry

Non-EU manufacturers wishing to register under REACH must appoint an Only Representative (OR) based in the EU. Many businesses discover this after their product has already entered the supply chain. TGC helps structure the OR arrangement and prepares registration dossiers to spec.

Deadline Management Across Jurisdictions

TSCA PFAS reporting in the US, K-REACH SDS updates in Korea (July 2026), UK REACH registration phases (2026 - 2030), and REACH 2.0 preparation in the EU - all on different timelines with different requirements. Without a dedicated compliance resource, these deadlines accumulate invisibly until they breach.

Supply Chain Transparency Under PFAS Scrutiny

PFAS are embedded across adhesives, coatings, packaging, and process chemicals throughout industrial supply chains. Mapping PFAS presence across your supplier base - to determine TSCA reporting scope, EU Annex XVII compliance, or customer disclosure obligations - requires systematic data collection that most internal teams lack capacity to conduct.

FAQs

Frequently Asked Questions About Chemical Compliance

Answers to the questions we hear most often from manufacturers, importers, and procurement teams entering new markets.

REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals) is the EU's primary chemical safety regulation, administered by ECHA. It applies to any company that manufactures, imports, or uses chemical substances in the EU in quantities of 1 tonne or more per year - unless exempt. Non-EU manufacturers who supply substances or mixtures into the EU must appoint an Only Representative (OR) based in the EU to fulfil registration obligations on their behalf. REACH also imposes disclosure obligations for Substances of Very High Concern (SVHCs) in articles above 0.1% w/w. A comprehensive revision - commonly referred to as REACH 2.0 - is under active development: the European Commission published its proposal in April 2025, but the final adoption date has slipped into 2026 or later, primarily due to a negative opinion from the EU Regulatory Scrutiny Board (RSB) in September 2025, which requires the impact assessment to be revised before the legislative proposal can proceed. Industry opposition at the April 2025 CARACAL consultation has added further pressure. Key proposed changes include 10-year registration validity, ECHA revocation powers for incomplete dossiers, stronger PFAS and endocrine disruptor controls, and polymer registration for up to 30,000 high-concern polymers.

REACH and CLP are complementary EU regulations that together form the EU's chemical safety framework. REACH governs the registration, evaluation, and authorisation of chemical substances - it is about ensuring chemicals are safe to use and that their risks are understood and managed. CLP (Classification, Labelling and Packaging Regulation) governs how hazardous substances and mixtures are classified, labelled, and packaged for sale - based on the UN's Globally Harmonised System (GHS). A substance can be subject to both regulations simultaneously: REACH may require it to be registered and evaluated, while CLP determines how it must be labelled on the shelf. Safety Data Sheets (SDS) are required under CLP/REACH and must follow the 16-section GHS format.

Yes. The US Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA) applies to any company that imports chemical substances or articles containing chemical substances into the United States - regardless of where the company is headquartered. Importers have obligations including verifying that imported substances appear on the TSCA Chemical Substance Inventory (or have a valid exemption), complying with use restrictions on regulated substances, and - from 2026 - reporting historical PFAS manufacture and import under TSCA Section 8(a)(7). Non-compliance can result in civil penalties exceeding $48,500 per day per violation (the statutory base of $37,500 under the 2016 Lautenberg Act is inflation-adjusted annually under 40 CFR § 19, reaching over $48,500 as of the 2024/2025 adjustment), product seizures, and enhanced import scrutiny from CBP and EPA.

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are a large family of synthetic chemicals used in firefighting foams, non-stick coatings, water-resistant textiles, packaging, and many industrial processes. They are described as "forever chemicals" because they do not break down in the environment or the human body, and have been detected in water supplies globally. In 2026, PFAS compliance has become a priority across multiple jurisdictions simultaneously: in the US, the TSCA PFAS reporting window opens in mid-2026 requiring manufacturers and importers to submit historical manufacturing data; in the EU, a comprehensive REACH Annex XVII restriction on PFAS is under active scientific review - ECHA's committees are expected to finalise their opinions through 2026, with a European Commission decision no earlier than 2027 and implementation potentially 2028 - 2029; ECHA is also advancing a broader restriction covering multiple sectors; in several US states, mandatory product labelling and reporting requirements are active. Companies with PFAS anywhere in their supply chain - including in articles - should conduct a PFAS mapping exercise as a first step.

A Safety Data Sheet (SDS) - formerly called a Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) - is a standardised document that provides detailed information about a chemical substance or mixture, including hazard identification, composition, safe handling, storage, and emergency response procedures. Under EU CLP/REACH, an SDS must be provided to downstream users and distributors for any hazardous substance or mixture classified under CLP, and for certain non-classified substances. The SDS must follow the 16-section GHS format. The specific language, pictogram, and content requirements vary by country: EU SDS requirements differ from OSHA HazCom 2012 requirements in the US, UK REACH requirements post-Brexit, and the Japanese ISHL or Korean GHS implementations. TGC's SDS management service covers authoring, review, translation, and market-specific format compliance.

REACH registration timelines and costs vary significantly by substance volume, hazard profile, and whether a lead registrant already exists in the SIEF (Substance Information Exchange Forum). Standard full registration for a new substance at 1 - 10 tonnes per year typically takes 6 - 18 months from scoping to submission, including test data gathering and dossier preparation. Letter of Access fees to existing SIEF data can range from a few hundred euros for common substances to tens of thousands for substances with limited registrants and proprietary data. ECHA registration fees are volume-banded and can be accessed on the ECHA website. Non-EU companies must appoint an Only Representative (OR) - a legal entity based in the EU - before any registration activity begins. TGC advises on SIEF participation, OR selection, and data sharing arrangements, and can coordinate the full registration process on your behalf.
