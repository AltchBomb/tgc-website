What We Handle

Cosmetic Compliance Across Every Market You Sell Into

Cosmetic compliance sits at the intersection of ingredient safety, labelling law, documentation, and registration - and every major market has its own framework. The EU Cosmetics Regulation, US MoCRA, UK OPSS, and the fragmented APAC landscape all impose different obligations, with different Responsible Person requirements, different ingredient annexes, and different enforcement timelines.

In 2024, cosmetics were the most frequently flagged product category in the EU Safety Gate Rapid Alert System - accounting for 36% of all 4,137 alerts, a record high. Of the chemical risk alerts for cosmetics, 97% involved the banned fragrance ingredient BMHCA - a substance prohibited since March 2022 that continues to appear in products placed on the EU market without adequate compliance checking. The enforcement environment is intensifying, and 2026 brings a wave of new ingredient deadlines across EU, UK, and US simultaneously.

TGC provides specialist cosmetic compliance support for brands, manufacturers, and importers operating across multiple markets. We combine regulatory expertise with practical product experience - covering everything from pre-launch ingredient screening and label review through to ongoing safety monitoring, Responsible Person services, and crisis response when compliance issues emerge.

We provide Label Reviews for INCI compliance and claims substantiation, Expedited Compliance Audits for urgent gap assessments, and Full Product Compliance management for brands entering multiple markets simultaneously.

Regulatory Landscape 2025-2026

What's Changing in Cosmetic Compliance

2025 and 2026 represent one of the most active regulatory periods for cosmetics in decades - ingredient bans, new enforcement frameworks, and MoCRA implementation all converging simultaneously. Here is what you need to know by region.

🇺🇸

United States - MoCRA

Active enforcement

MoCRA - Registration, Listing & Label Compliance Now Enforced

The Modernization of Cosmetics Regulation Act of 2022 (MoCRA) represents the most significant expansion of FDA cosmetics authority since 1938. Facility registration and product listing have been enforced since July 1, 2024. As of January 2025, there are 9,528 active facility registrations and 589,762 product listings in the FDA system. Contact information requirements on labels took effect December 29, 2024. MoCRA also gives FDA mandatory recall authority - the first time in US history the FDA can order a cosmetic recall without voluntary action.

Active

Adverse Event Reporting - Mandatory 15-Day Reporting

Under MoCRA, any serious adverse event - defined as an adverse health event associated with cosmetic use that results in hospitalisation, disfigurement, disability, or death - must be reported to the FDA within 15 business days. Records of all adverse events must be maintained for six years (three years for small businesses). Responsible Persons and manufacturers both carry reporting obligations. In December 2025, the FDA published draft guidance on mandatory cosmetic recalls, clarifying how it will exercise its new recall authority under MoCRA.

Approaching

cGMP Final Rule & State-Level Ingredient Bans

MoCRA directed the FDA to finalise binding Good Manufacturing Practice (cGMP) regulations for cosmetics by December 29, 2025. As of early 2026, the final rule has not yet been published - companies should follow ISO 22716 as the operative GMP standard in the interim. Separately, over 39 US states have introduced legislation targeting hazardous ingredients - California's Toxic-Free Cosmetics Act bans over 20 substances already restricted in the EU. Compliance with federal MoCRA does not guarantee compliance with state-level ingredient bans.

🇪🇺

European Union - EC 1223/2009

In force from Sept 2025

Omnibus VII - 22 CMR Substances Banned, No Transition Period

Regulation (EU) 2025/877 (Omnibus VII) entered into force on 1 September 2025, adding 22 substances newly classified as CMR (carcinogenic, mutagenic, or reprotoxic) to Annex II (prohibited ingredients) of the EU Cosmetics Regulation. Notably, Trimethylbenzoyl Diphenylphosphine Oxide (TPO) - a common photoinitiator in UV-cured gel and nail products - was banned with no transition period. Products containing affected substances cannot be placed on the EU market from 1 September 2025.

Applies from May 2026

Omnibus VIII - Further CMR Bans Including Perborate Derivatives

Regulation (EU) 2026/78, published 13 January 2026, adds further CMR substance restrictions to Annexes II, III, IV, and V of the EU Cosmetics Regulation - including perboric acid and its salts. The regulation applies from 1 May 2026 with no distinction between placing on market and making available - all non-compliant stock must be withdrawn by that date. Additionally, a Commission proposal (WTO notification May 2025) would ban 15 further CMR substances including Triphenyl Phosphate, with application from January 2027.

Active monitoring

Ongoing SCCS Opinions - Butylparaben, CBD, Titanium Dioxide

The SCCS has issued preliminary opinions flagging safety concerns for Butylparaben in products used by children under 10, and for CBD in cosmetics. A new SCCS mandate on Titanium Dioxide in oral care products is expected to drive further restrictions in 2026-2027. Brands using these ingredients should begin formulation review now. The EU is also evaluating a potential CMR reclassification of ethanol - a move that, if adopted, would have sweeping implications across fragrances, hand sanitisers, and personal care.

🇬🇧

United Kingdom - OPSS

Deadline July 2026

4-MBC Ban & Formaldehyde-Releaser Labelling - July 15, 2026

UK Statutory Instrument 2026 No. 23, laid before Parliament on 15 January 2026 by the Office for Product Safety and Standards (OPSS), introduces a complete ban on 4-Methylbenzylidene Camphor (4-MBC / Enzacamene), a UV filter previously used in sunscreens - effective 15 July 2026 (placing on market); products may remain on shelf until 14 January 2027. Stricter labelling requirements for formaldehyde-releasing preservatives also apply from 15 July 2026 - the trigger threshold for mandatory "releases formaldehyde" warnings drops from 0.05% to 0.001%.

Deadline Aug 2026

16 New CMR Substances Banned - August 15, 2026

The UK's 2026 cosmetics amendments add 16 CMR substances to Annex II of the UK Cosmetics Regulation, effective 15 August 2026. Banned substances include TPO, clothianidin, dimethyl propylphosphonate, dibutyltin maleate, tetrabromobisphenol-A, and others. Sell-through ends 15 February 2027. Source: SI 2026 No. 23 / OPSS.

Divergence risk

UK Operates Independently from EU - Separate RP & Notifications Required

Great Britain requires a separate UK-based Responsible Person (distinct from the EU RP), separate product notifications through the OPSS SCPN portal, and ongoing monitoring of UK-specific Statutory Instruments. Although the UK and EU are broadly aligned in ingredient policy direction, timelines and specific requirements increasingly diverge. Northern Ireland continues to follow EU cosmetics rules under the Windsor Framework.

🌏

Asia-Pacific - China, Korea, ASEAN

Active

China - NMPA Registration & Safety Assessment

China requires all cosmetic products to be registered or notified with the National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) before market entry. Ordinary cosmetics (most personal care items) require notification; special cosmetics (hair dye, sunscreen, hair growth, depilatory) require full registration - a more onerous process that can take 6-18 months. Animal testing exemptions are available for ordinary cosmetics sold only through online channels when supported by compliant safety assessment documentation. Products must appoint a China-based Responsible Person and display Chinese-language INCI ingredient lists.

Active enforcement

South Korea - MFDS Ingredient Testing Expanded Significantly

South Korea's Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS) increased testing of online-sold cosmetics from 110 items in 2024 to 1,080 in 2025 - a nearly tenfold increase - with particular focus on colour, eye, and nail products for harmful substances. Non-compliant products face sales bans and customs restrictions. Products must comply with Korea's Cosmetics Act and are subject to the Korea Restricted Ingredient List, which differs from EU and US frameworks. MFDS is also evaluating changes to natural and organic cosmetics certification standards.

Ongoing

ASEAN & Australia - Harmonisation with Divergence

ASEAN countries follow the ASEAN Cosmetic Directive (ACD), but national adoption and enforcement vary significantly. Thailand added 56 substances to its prohibited list in February 2025, aligning with the latest ACD update. In Australia, the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) enforces stricter oversight for products making therapeutic claims - cosmeceuticals and products marketed for anti-ageing or cellular repair are particularly scrutinised and may require TGA registration rather than cosmetics notification.

Why It Matters

The Cost of Poor Cosmetic Compliance Is Rising - Fast

The EU Safety Gate's record 4,137 alerts in 2024 - predominantly for cosmetics - reflects an enforcement environment that has fundamentally changed. Authorities are using the platform more aggressively, online marketplaces are under increased scrutiny, and the General Product Safety Regulation (GPSR), which came fully into effect in December 2024, introduces stricter traceability and recall obligations for all consumer products including cosmetics.

In the US, MoCRA enforcement is intensifying. As of January 2025, just 9,528 facilities were registered and 589,762 products listed in the FDA system - figures that represent a significant increase from the prior voluntary programme but still indicate many brands are non-compliant. The FDA's new mandatory recall authority means that a safety substantiation gap can now escalate directly to a forced recall, not just voluntary action.

The ingredient update cycle has also accelerated. In the EU alone, three binding annex updates have applied since September 2025, with two more in force by mid-2026. For brands without active regulatory monitoring in place, compliance can be lost silently - and discovered only when a shipment is flagged or a product is pulled from a retailer's shelves.

Real-World Scenario

Personal Care Brand - EU Shelf Pull & Retailer Contract Lost

A personal care brand exporting moisturisers and body lotions to three EU markets had its products pulled from retail shelves when routine market surveillance detected an undeclared fragrance allergen at concentrations above the Annex III reporting threshold. The product's INCI list had not been updated following a formulation change made by their contract manufacturer. The EU Responsible Person had not reviewed the Product Information File in over 18 months.

The consequence: €240,000 in lost inventory, emergency reformulation costs, PIF reconstruction by a qualified safety assessor, and the loss of a major retail distribution contract. The root cause was not a deliberate compliance failure - it was the absence of a systematic monitoring process between the brand, manufacturer, and Responsible Person. A quarterly compliance review would have identified the issue before any product reached the shelf.

Ingredient Caught in an Annex Update Mid-Cycle

A substance in your current formula is added to Annex II with no transition period. Without active regulatory monitoring, this is discovered at customs, on a retailer audit, or via a Safety Gate alert - not in advance. TGC monitors SCCS opinions and annex updates in real-time so reformulation conversations happen before deadlines, not after.

INCI Label Non-Compliance After Formulation Change

A contract manufacturer makes a formulation change without triggering a label review. The INCI list no longer reflects the formula. The allergen threshold in Annex III is now exceeded without declaration. This is one of the most common reasons products are flagged by EU Safety Gate - and one of the most preventable with structured change management.

📋

Outdated or Incomplete PIF

PIFs frequently fall out of date when formulations change, when ingredient restrictions are updated, or when the safety assessor's opinion becomes stale. A PIF that was compliant at launch may not be compliant today - and EU/UK authorities can request it at any time. TGC audits PIFs and manages ongoing updates as part of our Responsible Person service.

🌍

Entering EU and UK Simultaneously Without Separate RPs

Brands frequently discover that their EU Responsible Person cannot also act as UK RP - the two roles require separate legal entities in separate jurisdictions. Managing two distinct notification portals (CPNP and SCPN), two separate PIFs, and two diverging ingredient restriction timelines requires a coordinated, dual-market compliance programme.

🇺🇸

US MoCRA Registration Gap

Many international brands selling into the US via retail or e-commerce have not completed MoCRA facility registration or product listing - either because they were unaware of the requirement, or because their US distributor assumed responsibility but did not follow through. TGC assesses MoCRA compliance status and manages the registration and listing process.

💄

Claims That Cross Into Drug Territory

"Anti-ageing," "stimulates collagen production," "repairs cellular damage" - claims that are acceptable as cosmetic marketing in some markets can trigger pharmaceutical/drug classification in others. The EU, UK, US, and APAC each draw the line between cosmetic and medicinal claims differently. We review claims for each target market before they go to print.

FAQs

Frequently Asked Questions About Cosmetic Compliance

Answers to the questions we hear most often from cosmetic brands, manufacturers, and importers preparing for market entry or reviewing existing compliance programmes.

Under the EU Cosmetics Regulation (EC 1223/2009), a Responsible Person (RP) is a legal or natural person established within the EU who is designated for each cosmetic product placed on the EU market. The RP is legally accountable for compliance, including the Product Information File, safety assessment, labelling, and adverse event reporting. Non-EU manufacturers must appoint an EU-based entity as their Responsible Person before placing any product on the EU market.

The UK operates a separate, parallel system - a UK-based Responsible Person is required for Great Britain through OPSS. One entity cannot act as both EU RP and UK RP simultaneously, as each must be established in the respective jurisdiction. Brands expanding from EU into UK - or vice versa - must arrange separate RP appointments for each market.

MoCRA (Modernization of Cosmetics Regulation Act of 2022) is the most significant expansion of FDA cosmetics authority since 1938. Key requirements currently in force include: facility registration with the FDA (mandatory since July 1, 2024, renewed every two years); product listing for each marketed cosmetic through Cosmetics Direct; mandatory adverse event reporting (serious events within 15 business days); safety substantiation records; and contact information on all cosmetic labels (effective December 29, 2024).

As of January 2025, there were 9,528 active facility registrations and 589,762 product listings in the FDA system. MoCRA also grants FDA mandatory recall authority. The FDA published draft guidance on mandatory cosmetic recalls in December 2025, signalling an increasing enforcement posture.

A Product Information File (PIF) is a mandatory dossier required for every cosmetic product placed on the EU market. It must be kept by the Responsible Person and must include: a Cosmetic Product Safety Report (CPSR) prepared by a qualified safety assessor; the product description and formula; evidence of good manufacturing practice (GMP); details of any claims and supporting evidence; and records of adverse effects.

The PIF must be kept for 10 years from the date of last sale and made available to authorities on request. It must be updated whenever formulations change, when safety opinions are updated, or when ingredient regulations change - for example, when an ingredient is added to a restricted list. Failing to maintain a current PIF is one of the most common compliance failures identified in EU market surveillance. The UK has equivalent PIF requirements enforced by OPSS.

The EU cosmetic regulatory framework has been highly active. Omnibus VII (Regulation EU 2025/877), effective 1 September 2025, banned 22 CMR substances including TPO (used in UV-cured nail products) with no transition period. Omnibus VIII (Regulation EU 2026/78), published January 2026, applies from 1 May 2026 and adds further CMR bans including perborate derivatives.

A Commission proposal (WTO notification May 2025) would ban 15 further CMR substances including Triphenyl Phosphate, with application from January 2027 and sell-through until July 2028. Brands must also monitor ongoing SCCS opinions on Butylparaben (children), CBD, and Titanium Dioxide in oral care products - all of which are likely to drive further restrictions in 2026-2027.

Great Britain operates its own cosmetics regulation managed independently by OPSS, broadly retained from EU Cosmetics Regulation (EC 1223/2009) but updated via UK Statutory Instruments. Key differences: a separate UK-based Responsible Person is required; product notifications go through the UK SCPN portal (not EU's CPNP); and ingredient annex updates follow UK-specific timelines that increasingly diverge from the EU.

Key 2026 UK deadlines include the ban on 4-MBC UV filter (15 July 2026), stricter formaldehyde-releaser labelling (15 July 2026), and 16 new CMR substances banned from 15 August 2026. Northern Ireland continues to follow EU cosmetics rules under the Windsor Framework, meaning brands selling into Northern Ireland use EU requirements - not UK SI requirements.

Cosmetics were the most frequently flagged product category in the EU Safety Gate Rapid Alert System in 2024, accounting for 36% of a record 4,137 alerts - up from 32% in 2023. The primary driver is a single ingredient: BMHCA (2-(4-tert-butylbenzyl)propionaldehyde), a synthetic fragrance banned in the EU since March 2022, which accounted for 97% of chemical risk alerts for cosmetics in 2024. This indicates that many products - particularly those originating from China, which accounted for 40% of all Safety Gate alerts - are still entering the EU market containing this banned substance.

Other common failures include: prohibited or restricted ingredients above permitted limits; missing or incorrect INCI labelling; incomplete Responsible Person information; non-compliant cosmetic claims; and failure to update formulations following new Annex II/III restrictions. Source: European Commission 2024 Safety Gate Annual Report.
